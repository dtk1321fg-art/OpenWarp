# No-Go Theorems and Hard Limits

This file tracks the arguments that could block warp-drive physics.

OpenWarp should take these seriously. A good project does not ignore no-go results; it tries to understand exactly what assumptions they rely on.

## Energy Conditions

Energy conditions are rules in general relativity about what counts as physically reasonable matter/energy.

Important conditions:

- Null Energy Condition, NEC
- Weak Energy Condition, WEC
- Dominant Energy Condition, DEC
- Strong Energy Condition, SEC

Warp-drive metrics often require violations of one or more of these conditions.

## Quantum Inequalities

Quantum field theory allows local negative energy, but not freely.

Quantum inequalities restrict:

- how negative the energy can be
- how long it can last
- how it must be balanced by positive energy

This is one of the biggest problems for warp-drive ideas.

## Main Blocking Arguments

| Blocker | Meaning | Why it matters | Possible loophole |
|---|---|---|---|
| Exotic matter requirement | Original warp metrics require negative energy | No known source at useful scale | Different metrics, sublight cases, modified gravity |
| NEC violation | Generic warp drives may violate null energy condition | Usually considered unphysical or highly constrained | Identify exact assumptions and boundary cases |
| Quantum inequality limits | Negative energy is limited in amount/duration | Makes scaling extremely hard | New field states, configurations, or non-standard physics |
| Enormous energy requirements | Some models require astronomical energy | Engineering impossibility | Improved metrics, lower-speed cases, smaller test regions |
| Horizons and control | FTL bubbles may create horizons | Ship may not control the bubble from inside | Sublight experiments first |
| Stability | Bubble could collapse or behave unpredictably | Unsafe and non-engineerable | Dynamical simulations and stability constraints |
| Causality | FTL can imply time-travel-like paradoxes | Major theoretical problem | Avoid FTL; focus on sublight metric engineering |

## How to Analyze a No-Go Claim

For every blocking argument, record:

1. What exactly is being claimed?
2. What assumptions are required?
3. Does it apply to FTL only, or sublight too?
4. Does it apply to all metrics, or only one class?
5. Is it mathematical, experimental, or philosophical?
6. Has anyone challenged it?
7. What would count as a loophole?
8. What evidence would close the loophole?

## Rule for OpenWarp

If a proposed idea cannot explain how it avoids the known no-go arguments, it should be marked as weak until proven otherwise.
